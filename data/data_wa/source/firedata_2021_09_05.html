Incident Information -- Northwest Large Fire Information Summary

A Large Fire as defined by the National Wildland Coordinating Group, is any wildland fire in timber 100 acres or greater, and 300 acres or greater in grasslands/rangelands, or has an Incident Management Team assigned to it.

Information received from the National ICS-209 incident reporting database for large fires is updated each morning, and shows what has been reported by the Incident Commander the previous day at 7:00 PM. Information shown for each fire incident on the current day may not reflect changes made since the previous days ICS-209 input.

    During fire season, this web based interactive map is updated daily with current fire perimeters and locations. Access detailed information about these NW fires - current and historical. Status

9/4/20201

Current Status: Yesterday, fire activity was generally subdued by cloud cover and smoke conditions although
weather conditions continue to get warmer and dryer in the region. The fire remains active on the northwest side
in the Mother Lode and Welcome Creek drainages moving towards the Bull of the Woods Lookout. Helicopter
bucket water drops were used to slow fire movement in key areas, such as near the lookouts. Indirect lines
southwest of the fire area are complete and ready to be used. Along the southeast edge and the ridge at the top
of Humbug Creek containment lines were patrolled and strengthened. The tactical firing operation along the
eastern side of the fire and Rhododendron Ridge continued to build depth under favorable wind and weather
conditions. The containment lines on the northeast side of the fire at the confluence of the Collawash River and
Elk Lake Creek were patrolled and strengthened. Several miles of indirect lines have been prepared north and
northwest of the fire area. Additional work was done to remove material from those lines.

Planned Actions: Firefighters will test hose lines, pumps and tanks on the indirect lines that have been prepared.
Crews will strengthen existing containment lines and mop up hot spots where possible. On the eastern side fire
crews will mop up along the edge of the recent burning and with the expected wind shift will focus on holding that
line. Removal of hazard trees will also be done to improve safety and access. Fire activity is expected to
increase at midday when the temperature inversion lifts. Helicopters will continue to be used to slow fire spread
in key areas on the northwest side of the fire and other areas as necessary. Large volumes of smoke are
expected due to the amount of vegetation burning in the fire.

The weather today will be warm and dry with temps from the mid 70s to lower 80 degrees, humidity levels
dropping to the lower teens to mid 20s, and winds out of the west/ southwest from 4-9 mph with gusts up to 12-
15 mph. Warmer and dryer conditions are forecasted to continue for the next several days.

Evacuations: The Marion County Sheriff has issued a Level 1 “Be Ready” evacuation advisory only for
Breitenbush Hot Springs. Residents should be aware of the danger that exists in their area, monitor emergency
services websites and local media, and make an action plan should the evacuation level rise. The advisory can
be viewed at: https://www.flashalert.net/id/MarionSheriff?alert=1.

Closures: For the safety of wildland firefighters and the public, the National Forests have enacted area closures
that prevent the public from being upon National Forest System lands in the vicinity of the Bull Complex where
uncontrolled fire and fire suppression activities are occurring. All Forest lands that are south and west of Forest
Road 46, and east of the Riverside Fire perimeter are closed. The entire length of Forest Road 46 is closed.
Lands within the 2020 Lionshead and Beachie Creek Fire areas are also closed. See the closure orders and
maps at https://inciweb.nwcg.gov/incident/closures/7773/.

Contractors approved by the Forest Service through a permit system are allowed through the closure order, as
are vendors and others working on recovery efforts related to the 2020 area fires. If the fire continues to grow to
the south, some operations may be put on hold.

Air Quality: For information on air quality and related health concerns, visit https://fire.airnow.gov/ or
https://oregonsmoke.blogspot.com/.

Primary and Cooperating Agencies: U.S. Forest Service, Oregon Department of Forestry, Bureau of Land
Management, Marion County Sheriff’s Office, Clackamas County Sheriff’s Office.

9/2/2021 Update

Kettle Falls, WA – Northwest Incident Management Team 7 will transfer command of the Bulldog Mountain and
Mack Mountain Fires to the Colville National Forest tomorrow at 0700. This will be Northwest Team 7’s last
update.

Yesterday, firefighters conducted a successful burning operation on the southern flank of the Bulldog Mountain
Fire with assistance from air resources. Crews intend on burning along the southern flank again today to close
the gap between the fire’s edge and South Boulder Road.

Crews will continue to patrol and mop up along the northern flank and take actions to hold the remainder of the
fire within containment lines. The eastern flank is in patrol status.

Air and firefighting resources will monitor both the Bulldog Mountain and Mack Mountain Fires for active fire
behavior, while crews continue road grading on Boulder Cabin Road and other roads around the fires for the next
few days.

The cold front that moved into the area on Monday night brought cooler weather, higher relative humidity, and
northwest winds with the possibility of light showers.

Citizens should remain vigilant and are strongly encouraged to sign up for alerts at Ferry County Alerts.
Motorists traveling on Boulder Hwy and SR395 should watch for heavy equipment and firefighters turning off and
onto the roadway. Firefighter and public safety is our number one priority.

Smoke: Current weather conditions will moderate smoke impacts throughout the area today.

Closures: An area closure is in effect on Colville National Forest lands in the vicinity of the Bulldog Mountain Fire.
For more information on area and road closures on the Colville National Forest, please visit:
https://www.fs.usda.gov/Internet/FSE_DOCUMENTS/fseprd938379.pdf

Citizens are asked to stay clear of the fire area and comply with area closures.

While current weather conditions provide respite from the extreme fire potential present in northeast Washington,
the forest can quickly dry out again in the many weeks of fire season ahead. Please continue to do all you can to
prevent wildfires.

The Colville National Forest is at IFPL level 3. Road closures remain in effect.

Status

9/3/2021 Update

The Devil's Knob Complex is a group of lightning-caused fires on the Tiller Ranger District of the Umpqua
National Forest and private lands protected by Douglas Forest Protective Association and the Cow Creek
Band of the Umpqua Tribe of Indians. Two significant fires remain active: the Smith mostly to the north
and the Big Hamlin to the south of the South Umpqua River, with interior pockets burning and most
natural fire growth occurring at a moderate rate on the Smith Fire’s north and east sides.

Smith Fire (26,542 acres): On the northwest edge, crews continue building containment lines north to
Forest Service Road 2719, engaging in direct attack when feasible and conducting strategic firing
operations. In Ash Valley, crews are well prepared to protect private property in the community as the
fire nears management evaluation points required to enact the structure protection plan. On the east
side, crews continue to prepare for strategic firing operations working northeast toward Forest Service
Road 27. On the south side, hand crews continue mop-up operations in the Zinc Creek area.

Big Hamlin Fire (15,793 acres): Firefighters continue making steady progress on the east side with
strategic firing operations along Forest Service Road 2925. Crews also continue to build containment
lines along the east and northeast perimeter near Fawn Creek and Whisky Creek. Containment lines on
the west side continue holding well, and firefighters are engaged in mop-up and patrol operations along
the fire’s southern edges.

Dismal Creek, Little Applegate, Mule Creek, Section 30 (Railroad) and Wild Cat fires (1,495 total acres):
These fires are contained and remain in patrol status. Any change in acreage is because of better
mapping.

Weather: Warm and dry conditions will continue through the weekend with high temperatures in the
80s. Smoky skies will moderate fire behavior but also limit the ability to conduct air operations. Winds
will remain light out of the northwest around 6 mph in the lower elevations.

Air Quality: Check latest conditions on the Oregon Smoke Information Blog: http//oregonsmoke.blogspot.com.

Status

9/3/2021

Westfir, OR – Northwest Team 6 had a successful shadow day yesterday and assumed command at 7:00 AM
today. Yesterday, members traveled to the line to finalize understanding of operational plans and will continue
and build upon Northwest Team 13’s plan moving forward. Crews, air operations, and ground support largely
remain unchanged through the transition.

The Gales Fire slowly progressed Friday as crews continued lining, clearing, and strategically burning the
north/northeast flank of the fire. Retardant was deployed earlier this week on the ridge east of the fire, and today
crews are working on connecting key pieces of line by burning flammable vegetation between the fire and
established control line.

Containment efforts of the spot fires in the Purdue, Delp, Portland, and Never-Go Creek drainages are supported
with aircraft bucket drops and are being closely monitored.

Active mop up on the Gales Fire in certain areas and on the Kwis Fire strengthens and improves containment
line, or the “black” on the maps.

Weather: A weak cold front moved through on Friday afternoon. The afternoon cloud cover shielded the fire area
from the sun’s heat and dampened fire activity during the peak temperatures of the day. Today, the weather will
continue to be warm and dry, but a stable inversion is expected to cause smoke to settle low over the area and
remain later into the day than normal. While this will cause poor air quality in the area, it will also have the effect
of moderating fire behavior.

Closures and Recreation: There are numerous fire closures on national forests throughout the Pacific Northwest.
An interactive map showing all Forest Service closures can be

found at tinyurl.com/4j3fxy8y. Fire danger is very high, and fire restrictions are still in place, including restrictions
on campfires and charcoal grills. Check before you go.

Status

9/4/2021 Update

Highlights: Firefighters are focused on securing containment lines in several critical locations around the fire
area. Limited burning operations are planned in those areas that present the greatest threat for fire spread
beyond constructed firelines.

Under favorable weather conditions, crews will ignite vegetation to reduce the amount of fuel ahead of the main
fire in multiple locations, including a small area northeast of Bitterroot Mountain and a second area heading west
toward Lynx Mountain. Crews will place fire hoses and pumps along existing handlines and dozerlines to further
secure these areas and prevent fire spread to the southeast. On the far north end of the fire, crews will
strategically apply fire in an isolated area westof Stall Creek. The public should expect to see increased smoke
as a result of these planned burning operations. Aviation resources are providing water drops to assist
firefighters in reducing fire activity in these areas.

Operations: In addition to the planned burning operations, fire crews are responding to increased heat observed
in the area south of Brush Mountain. Although this spot fire has been inactive for several days, fire managers are
sending three engines to investigate and extinguish any remaining hot spots. As warmer and drier weather
trends persist, smoke may become more visible in areas within the fire perimeter. On the southern portion of the
fire where activity is minimal, firefighters are monitoring the perimeter by air to allow ground resources to focus
on the most active areas. On the northwest side, the fire crossed the West Fork of Hall Creek and is moving
upslope from South Seventeenmile Mountain. Firefighters are developing a strategy to halt the fire’s progress in
this challenging terrain that includes dense pine regeneration.

Weather: A passing weather system will bring increased clouds today, although this cloud cover will have little
influence on temperatures or humidity with warm and dry conditions still expected. The prevailing wind will be
south to southwest at speeds generally less than 10 mph; however, some higher gusts are possible in north to
south oriented valleys and drainages.Warm and dry weather is expected through early next week.

Safety: The health and safety of firefighters and the public is the first priority. The public is strongly encouraged to
stay safely clear of the active fire area. Firefighters are conducting operations under protocols to mitigate the risk

9/4/2021 Update

Yesterday: Cloud cover blanketed the fire in the afternoon but warm temperatures and continuing very
dry weather resulted in active burning in pockets of unburned vegetation in the Twenty-five Mile Creek
drainage in the northwest area of fire. That burning produced a smoke column that was visible to
surrounding communities but posed no threat to containment lines. Helicopters dropped water on areas
of active burning on the western edge of the fire to delay fire spread.

On the North side of the fire, crews continued mop-up operations to extinguish heat near fire lines
around Communications Site 911 and from Utah Saddle to Chesapeake Saddle and Junior Point. Late
in the day conditions allowed further aerial and hand ignition to add depth along containment lines
toward Junior Spike. To the south, firefighters mopped up where previous days’ burning occurred along
Forest Road 8410 and dozer lines west of Slide Peak. Fire managers also assessed opportunities to
adjust and improve containment lines for future firing operations southwest-west towards Stormy
Mountain.

Today: Heavy equipment will be used to repair Forest Road 8410 and roads near Shady Pass for fire
traffic safety and to begin modifications of some containment lines towards Slide Peak. Modifications
will enhance future firing and holding operations. Containment lines from Twenty-five Mile State Park to
Utah Saddle are in patrol status given the lack of active fire along these containment lines. Engines and
firefighters will continue to hold, mop-up, and improve the fire line between Utah Saddle and Junior
Spike on the North and around Slide Ridge on the South.

As conditions allow, firefighters will continue to implement burning operations to strengthen
containment lines towards Stormy Mountain and from Junior Spike to Crescent mountain. Structure
assessments are complete along the Lake Chelan corridor and First Creek subdivision. Firefighters will
continue to test out the operability of the network of pumps and hoselays in these areas should they be
needed.

Weather and Fire Behavior: More clouds are expected today as a weak front moves through the area.
This will bring breezy conditions by afternoon, but relative humidity minimums will still approach 20
percent. As smoke in the afternoon lifts and winds increase, fire activity is expected to produce visible
smoke again today as pockets and stringers of vegetation ignite. Smoky conditions may persist
decreasing air quality and air support for suppression activities.
