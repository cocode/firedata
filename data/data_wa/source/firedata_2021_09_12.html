Incident Information -- Northwest Large Fire Information Summary

A Large Fire as defined by the National Wildland Coordinating Group, is any wildland fire in timber 100 acres or greater, and 300 acres or greater in grasslands/rangelands, or has an Incident Management Team assigned to it.

Information received from the National ICS-209 incident reporting database for large fires is updated each morning, and shows what has been reported by the Incident Commander the previous day at 7:00 PM. Information shown for each fire incident on the current day may not reflect changes made since the previous days ICS-209 input.

Current Status: Yesterday, a small amount of rain fell over the fire area with 0.10” measured on the west side to

Creek, and Battle drainages. The fire slowly backed downslope in the Elk Lake Creek and East Humbug Creek

north and south containment lines. Fire crews worked to prepare road systems and hand lines north and
northwest of the fire area to be primary or contingency lines as part of the long-term fire suppression strategy.
Preparation work is being done along an estimated 40 miles of forest roads. On the southwest side a hand line
was constructed to connect the road system to the Beachie Creek Fire scar to be used as an anchor point. Along
the southeast side firefighters patrolled the line. Extensive mop up work was done along Forest Road 6350 on
the east side of the fire as crews work to secure that edge to keep the fire within containment line. The two spot
fires east of the line were monitored and remain secured.

Planned Actions: Under moderated weather conditions fire activity is expected to be less active but still expected
to creep, smolder, and have occasional group tree torching because fuel conditions remain extremely dry. The
fire will continue to move in the Upper Dickey Creek, Mother Lode Creek, Pansey Creek, and Battle Creek
drainages and back downslope in the East Humbug Creek drainage. Helicopters remain on standby to be used
for water bucket drops if cloud and smoke conditions improve. Existing containment lines will be monitored as
firefighters watch for any trees or pockets of heat that could threaten the lines. Mop up work and hazard tree
removal will be done along Forest Road 6350 to secure the fire line. On the south side firefighters will patrol and
monitor existing line and work on a hand line to the southwest. Crews and equipment will be used to prepare
lines using several road systems northwest and north of the fire area.

Weather: The weather today is expected to get warmer and drier. Temperatures will be 67-74 degrees, minimum
humidity levels from 38% to 48%, and winds out of the west/northwest at 5-10 mph with gusts up to 15 mph

After yesterday’s significant rainfall, fire activity was relatively quiet. Night shift crews and heavy equipment
completed contingency lines in the southwest portion of the fire to protect private timber lands. Today, firefighters
are scouting and prepping line, continuing north up the west side of the fire in anticipation of drying fuels for burn
operations in the area. On the north portion, crews are tying in light fuels in the valley to minimize impacts, and
cold trailing. On the east flank, dozers and crews are constructing direct line to protect additional private timber
land. Moving south on the east flank, direct line is also being constructed to tie into previously constructed line in
the south to secure the fire’s edge. Firefighters will be looking at opportunities for indirect burning operations in
the next few days while weather conditions are favorable to do so. There was minimal fire growth reported
yesterday and the increase is likely from burning that happened prior to the rain.

The Oregon State Fire Marshal’s office currently has six taskforces working during the day and four taskforces
working at night. Key focuses for firefighters today will be mopping up near structures on the eastern and
northeastern flanks of the fire. Crews will also be working today to assess homes on the westside of the fire.

Another key focus for structural firefighters will be the protection of critical infrastructure on Round Mountain. The
mountain is home to FFA communication, internet and cellular tower services that are vital to the area.

Behavior:Fine fuel moistures today will be in the 6-8% range. Low fire behavior is expected through all fuel types
with creeping surface fire and single or small group torching under protected canopies in timber. Afternoon
spread will be possible in light fuels by late afternoon.

Weather: Today will have a light haze and a few afternoon clouds. Temperatures in the valleys will be 71-76
degrees, with a relative humidity of 19-23%. Winds will be light and variable in the morning, becoming north-
northwest in the afternoon at 5-8 mph with gusts less than 15 mph.

In some years, the amount of rain we have received at this time of year could be considered a season ending
event. This season, however, is not a typical season. This area is in its second consecutive year of drought and
entered this fire season in the highest classification of drought measure possible. Likewise, fuel moistures have
been at or near record lows for most of the year. Just as yesterday’s rains will not fill our empty reservoirs, it will
also not automatically bring an end to this fire season.

9/11/2021 Update
Current Situation:

Yesterday’s reduced fire behavior continued a much-needed reprieve, allowing firefighters to connect and
strengthen firelines and increase containment on both fires. The beneficial weather allowed crews to directly
engage fire edges where fire activity was dampened. Firing operations were minimal as fuels need to be dry
enough to carry fire. Firefighters depend on fine fuels such as grasses and pine needles to help get firing
operations established; when humidity is high and temperatures are low, moisture in fine fuels is increased
making it difficult to carry fire. Heavy fuels, such as dead and down logs, take longer to absorb water, and
therefore hold fire longer.

Today, firefighters will continue to use weather conditions to their advantage by working directly on the fire edges
and strengthening firelines as conditions allow. Today’s weather will exemplify a typical September day in the
Southern Cascade Range with temperatures around 70 degrees, high humidity, and mild winds. Fire conditions
and operational activities will be largely unchanged from yesterday because the stable weather will provide for
mild burning conditions and limited fire spread, allowing fire fighters to continue their work.

Firefighters will maintain their strong presence in Ash Valley, securing firelines around structures. Highway 28

Big Hamlin Fire
Acreage: 19,035 | Containment: 75%

Fireline suppression repair will continue in fully extinguished sections with patrol and mop-up ongoing where heat

Weather and Fuel Conditions:
Cloudy conditions will remain over the fire until this afternoon, limiting fire spread. Once clouds move out,

expected to remain for the next several days and will result in fire activity that mirrors today. Areas with heavy
dead and downed trees, and underneath tree canopies where moisture didn’t penetrate, may experience tree
torching and some active fire.

Incident Summary: The Great Basin Incident Management Team 2 took command of the Rough Patch Complex
and Jack Fires at 6:00 a.m. Both fires are being managed under a full suppression strategy using containment
and confinement tactics to minimize fire spread to prevent loss of valuable resources. Objectives include
keeping the fires as small as possible while working toward full containment.

Weather in the area is forecast to be cooler and more humid. Some precipitation was received across the fire
Thursday night, with .10 to .30 inches of rain received in some locations. It is predicted to remain at these
seasonably lower temperatures for the next few days. Weather conditions east of the Cascades could bring more
humidity and cloud cover to the fire area, perhaps even some drizzle on Friday. These changes reflect the typical
patterns for this time of year and may have the potential to diminish fire activity, especially if prolonged over
several days.

Chaos and Little Bend Fires: The Chaos fire is at 24,462 acres and the Little Bend Creek Fire is at 8,569 acres.
On the north end of the Chaos Fire, firefighters assessed the spot fire that crossed the 2460 road. It is currently
burning at approximately three acres. With the very steep and rugged landscape in mind, crews have identified

to support this priority with water drops and reconnaissance flights. On the southeast section of the Little Bend
Creek Fire, crews are utilizing the 3816 road as a primary control line after the fire crossed over a section of the

corner of the Buckhead Fire near the 100 road where they have contained small spot fires that resulted also

Near Minky Fire: The Near Minky Fire is at 4,867 acres. This fire lays south of the Jack and Buckhead Fires.

Did you know? Yesterday’s topic talked about the different positions on an Incident Management Team. Over the
next few days, we will take a few of those positions each day and talk about what their role looks like. Incident
Commander: This role is responsible for the overall objectives and safety of the team, personnel, and the fire. In
the private sector this person would be the “Mayor” of the city. The Incident Commander (IC) handles the political
aspects of the fire as far as keeping local leaders and other agencies apprised of what’s going on. Operations
Section Chief: This person is responsible for the overall operations on the actual fire. He/she has a field
operations person that is working with the crews in the field on the actual fire. Field Operations reports each day
to the Operations Section Chief. They collaborate on resource needs, objectives and what’s needed to move
forward on extinguishment and containment of the fire. This team is critical to fire control and safety of the
firefighters on the ground. Stay tuned for tomorrows brief when we talk about 2 more roles.

Incident Summary: The Great Basin Incident Management Team 2 took command of the Rough Patch Complex
and Jack Fires at 6:00 a.m. Both fires are being managed under a full suppression strategy using containment
and confinement tactics to minimize fire spread to prevent loss of valuable resources. Objectives include
keeping the fires as small as possible while working toward full containment.

Weather in the area is forecast to be cooler and more humid. Some precipitation was received across the fire
Thursday night, with .10 to .30 inches of rain received in some locations. It is predicted to remain at these
seasonably lower temperatures for the next few days. Weather conditions east of the Cascades could bring more
humidity and cloud cover to the fire area, perhaps even some drizzle on Friday. These changes reflect the typical
patterns for this time of year and may have the potential to diminish fire activity, especially if prolonged over
several days.

On the north end of the Chaos Fire, firefighters assessed the spot fire that crossed the 2460 road. It is currently
burning at approximately three acres. With the very steep and rugged landscape in mind, crews have identified
specific terrain features they are using in efforts to contain the spot and minimize its growth. Helicopters continue
to support this priority with water drops and reconnaissance flights. On the southeast section of the Little Bend
Creek Fire, crews are utilizing the 3816 road as a primary control line after the fire crossed over a section of the
3850 road on Tuesday.

Jack and Buckhead Fires: The Jack Fire is at 23,990 acres and the Buckhead fire is at 6,536 acres. The Jack
Fire is contained along the west, north, and northeast perimeter. The fire has not grown for several days. Fire
crews are assessing the eastern edge for direct control operations. Firefighters are focusing on the southwest
corner of the Buckhead Fire near the 100 road where they have contained small spot fires that resulted also
during Tuesday’s wind event. Due to the proximity of the Smith Fire, which is part of the Devil’s Knob Complex,
close coordination is ongoing between the two Incident Management Teams managing each complex.

Near Minky Fire: The Near Minky Fire is at 4,867 acres. This fire lays south of the Jack and Buckhead Fires.

9/11/2021 Update

The Summit Trail Fire received a half-inch of rain overnight, reducing fire activity throughout the fire area. Light
rain showers persisted for nearly 12 hours, allowing small fuels such as dead grass, pine needles, and small
sticks and twigs to absorb enough moisture that they will likely require several days of lower humidity and wind to
dry out before supporting active fire movement. Larger fuels such as branches, limbs, and snags may still
continue to burn despite these wetter conditions. Spot fires are likely to surface in the coming days as the
smoldering debris on the forest floor dries out and reignites.

Operations: On the northwest side of the fire, crews evaluated the burnout operation from the previous day and
attempted to apply more fire to the inside fire edge to increase burnout depth. Due to the high humidity, the
vegetation was not receptive in this area, so crews redirected their efforts to mop up material near the edge of
the fire and move it more interior, working their way across the north side of the fire towards Stall Creek.
Southeast of Stall Creek, firefighters completed their planned ignitions and contained spots that were threatening
existing handlines. On the northeastern flank, crews discovered and suppressed a 1/3-acre spot fire that had
crossed the line. Patrols continue on the east side near Lynx Mountain, Brush Mountain, and Bear Springs, and
along the southern portions of the fire using a combination of air and ground resources. Although visibility and
weather conditions have limited the use of air support, these aircraft remain available and ready to respond if
needed. Fire managers have created a Suppression Repair Group that will begin working to repair culverts and
roads, remove log decks from control lines, and remove any existing equipment that is no longer needed around
the fire area.

Weather: Light showers may linger early this morning but should clear the region before midday. The sun will
return this afternoon, but clouds will increase late in the day ahead of another weather system. Temperatures will
remain near seasonal normals and afternoon humidity will stay high. The rest of the weekend through early next
week will be partly cloudy and seasonably mild. Cool and breezy conditions will follow a cold front on
Wednesday.

Safety: The health and safety of firefighters and the public is the first priority. The public is strongly encouraged to
stay safely clear of the active fire area. Firefighters are conducting operations under protocols to mitigate the risk
of COVID-19.

along Devil’s Backbone. Heavy fuels were receptive to burning, however additional burning may be required to

line), and securing the line. Crews also made good progress completing road work needed to ensure safe
