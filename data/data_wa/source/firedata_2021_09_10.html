Incident Information -- Northwest Large Fire Information Summary

A Large Fire as defined by the National Wildland Coordinating Group, is any wildland fire in timber 100 acres or greater, and 300 acres or greater in grasslands/rangelands, or has an Incident Management Team assigned to it.

Information received from the National ICS-209 incident reporting database for large fires is updated each morning, and shows what has been reported by the Incident Commander the previous day at 7:00 PM. Information shown for each fire incident on the current day may not reflect changes made since the previous days ICS-209 input.

Status

9/9/20201

Current Status: As some of the smoke cleared yesterday afternoon, the fire activity increased on the northwest
side of the fire in the Upper Dickey Creek, Mother Lode Creek, and Pansey Creek drainages. Smoke was visible
as the fire made some uphill runs in those areas located within the Bull of the Woods Wilderness. The fire
activity did not threaten any fire lines or structures. Along the southwest side crews worked on some hand line
construction and strengthened primary lines on the road system. Crews monitored the fire as it backed down the
north side of the Elk Lake Creek drainage and the upper part of the East Humbug Creek drainage towards
established fire lines. The south and north established containment lines were patrolled and monitored for hot
spots. Firefighters mopped up along Forest Road 6350 and monitored fire movement on the southeast corner
where it has burned up to the Lionshead Fire scar. Mop up work involves putting out any flames or heat along
the perimeter and containing the fire by working a specified distance in from the edge to make sure that the fire
won’t increase in size along that edge. Fire crews worked to prepare road systems and hand lines north and
northwest of the fire area to be primary or contingency lines as part of the long-term fire suppression strategy.

Planned Actions: Fire activity is expected to remain active in the Upper Dickey Creek, Mother Lode Creek, and
Pansey Creek areas which could increase smoke during the day. Firefighters will remain alert for changing
conditions. On the south side crews will continue to prepare primary lines utilizing road systems. Helicopter
bucket water drops will be used to slow fire spread in key areas. Firefighters will patrol the north and south
existing containment lines and make sure they remain secure. Along the eastern side firefighters will mop up
along Forest Road 6350 and patrol both the northeast and southeast corners. Equipment and crews will reduce
fuels along the road systems northwest of the fire area. Reducing fuels is work done to reduce the density of
vegetation along a road, trail, or constructed line to make a good line of defense which under normal conditions
can slow an advancing fire and reduce fire intensity to provide a safety margin for firefighters to work.

Weather: Warm and dry conditions will likely persist today. Temperatures will be 70-78 degrees, minimum
humidity levels at 31% to 42%, and winds out of the southwest 3-7 mph with gusts up to 20 mph.
Evacuations: The Marion County Sheriff has issued a Level 1 “Be Ready” evacuation advisory only for
Breitenbush Hot Springs. Residents should be aware of the danger that exists in their area, monitor emergency
services websites and local media, and make an action plan should the evacuation level rise. The advisory can
be viewed at: https://www.flashalert.net/id/MarionSheriff?alert=1.

Closures: For the safety of wildland firefighters and the public, the National Forests have enacted area closures
that prevent the public from being upon National Forest System lands in the vicinity of the Bull Complex Fire.
All Forest lands that are south and west of Forest Road 46, and east of the Riverside Fire perimeter are closed.
The entire length of Forest Road 46 is closed. Lands within the 2020 Lionshead and Beachie Creek Fire areas
are also closed. See the closure orders and maps at https://inciweb.nwcg.gov/incident/closures/7773/.

Air Quality: For information on air quality, visit https://fire.airnow.gov/ or https://oregonsmoke.blogspot.com/.

Primary and Cooperating Agencies: U.S. Forest Service, Oregon Department of Forestry, Bureau of Land
Management, Marion County Sheriff’s Office, Clackamas County Sheriff’s Office.

Status

9/8/2021 Update

Current Situation:
Fire managers were prepared for a busy day yesterday due to hot conditions and low humidity, preceding the
arrival of a low pressure system, coupled with uncertainties about additional smoke from California. The smoke
covering did not arrive, which resulted in large smoke columns, spot fires across several firelines on the north
and east sides, and approximately 2 miles of growth on the northeastern side of the Smith Fire. This required a
defensive approach to firefighting actions with a focus on containing spot fires. In the evening, several hours of
increased winds with gusts up to 25 mpg contributed to sustained active fire behavior into the night. Some crews’
shifts were extended allowing for continued engagement with spot fires late into the night.

Today, increased humidity and smoke shading will provide some reprieve to firefighting efforts. Firefighter’s
actions will remain on the defensive to catch spot fires and secure fire edges. In the coming days, stable weather
resembling a normal pattern will calm fire behavior allowing crews to resume direct and indirect line construction
supported with firing operations where needed.

Smith Fire
Acreage: 39,579
Branch I: Divisions E, F and G – In Divisions F and G, work accomplished earlier in the week remained secure.
Several spot fires were detected in Division G, requiring the attention of 3 hotshot crews; these will remain the
focus of today’s work until they are contained. Firefighters will continue to patrol contained sections of Divisions
E, F, and G to ensure containment lines are not compromised.
Division A – Progress was made on prepping firelines and planning continued for tying in firelines between the
Smith and Near Minky Fires. Much of the Smith Fire’s growth yesterday occurred in this area, moving the fire
closer to the Near Minky Fire; these fires are approximately 2 miles apart. Firefighter safety remains the top
priority as managers identify ways to secure firelines on both fires.
Branch II: Division B – Multiple spot fires occurred in Ash Valley, late yesterday afternoon, due to weather
conditions and very dry fuels which allowed for active fire behavior. Due to the fire’s advance towards Ash Valley,
firefighters had to conduct some defensive firing to protect the homes in this area. The contingency actions of the
last few days along with the defensive actions of firefighters yesterday paid off as no structures were lost. Today,
crews will continue structure protection and to line spot fires, mop up, secure fire perimeters, and will resume
building fire line to Forest Road 28, if conditions allow.
Divisions C and E – Fire perimeters in these divisions remain secure with patrols and mop-up continuing today.

Big Hamlin Fire
Acreage: 18,814
Branch III: Divisions J, L, and N – Actions of the last several days will continue, which include patrol and mop-up
along firelines and commencement of suppression repair activities where feasible.
Divisions H – Several spot fires occurred within this division yesterday. Today firefighters will secure known spot
fires and patrol looking for any additional spot fires; once these actions are complete patrol and mop-up of fire
edges will resume.

Weather and Fuel Conditions:
Temperatures will moderate after yesterday’s weather although temperatures will be above normal for this time of
year, reaching 80 degrees for most locations. Winds will be light, but may be strong enough to cause partial
clearing of the smoke. Fire behavior is expected to still be active today, although to a lesser degree than
yesterday.

The Gales Fire is currently 11% contained. The term containment indicates what percent of the fire perimeter has
been surrounded by a control line that prevents fire advancement. On the maps, red indicates uncontained fire
perimeter, and black is contained fireline. Lines have been constructed surrounding the Gales Fire, but fire

by removing burnable material near the lines. Recently completed strategic burns on the southwest and
northeast flanks of the fire are being monitored and will be extended to keep the main fire in check.

The Kwis Fire is 98% contained. Crews continue to patrol and pull out any unnecessary equipment and hose,
and repair efforts are now underway on many of the lines that were built to contain this fire.

Temperatures will be at or below seasonal norms Thursday and Friday.

Smoke: Offshore flow from the west will push out a lot of the smoke this afternoon, but fires to the south continue
to send smoke that will hang aloft.

Status

9/7/2021 Update

Conconully, WA: The Muckamuck Fire started by lightning on Saturday August 4, 2021. The fire is located in and
around the Town of Conconully, Washington. The fire is in an area that has not burned in recent years. Mixed
conifer with dead and downed materials are the primary fuel source along with ponderosa pine, sagebrush, and
cured grass meadows. The fire has held at 13,314 acres and is now 74% contained. During the first phase of the
fire there were 4 primary and 3 secondary structures lost. The fire is located on lands managed by the Colville
National Forest. There are trails, recreation areas, and road closures in effect.

Today the fire was turned over from Northwest Interagency Incident Management Team 9 (Type 2) to Northeast
Washington Interagency Incident Management Team 2 (Type 3). The transition was seamless and Team 2
appreciated their hard work during the transition setting the Type 3 team up for success. Please note the updated
contact and additional website information.

Primary containment lines around the fire are holding however, contingent containment lines are in place as a
normal course of action with a fire this size. Objectives over the next few days are continue securing indirect fire
line, mopping up 150 feet in from perimeter, repairing dozer lines, while hand crews continue to repair handlines.
Defensive measures are still in place for interior burning and grass areas that were not burned during the initial
fire. Air resources will be used in areas that are difficult to reach or areas that need immediate attention.

Temperatures will warm slightly as the valleys are expected to hit the high 80s and mid 70s in the higher terrain.
Humidity is expected to be in the high teens and low 20s over the next few days. A mild weather system is
expected later in the week with minimal amounts of rain forecasted (less than 1/10 of an in.) and minimal wind.
Winds are expected to be terrain driven in the up-slope areas all week. Today is expected to be the warmest of
the week.

There are road closures and restrictions in place. Be sure to check before you travel to the area for a drive or
hike. Citizens that are hiking and hunting have come in contact with firefighters over the last few days. There are
several hazards that have been created from the fire. For your safety and the safety of our firefighters we ask
you to stay clear of the area.

FS Road 37 and FS Road 42 remain closed at the junction
FS Road 38 is closed at the forest boundary north of Conconully
Mineral Hill Road is closed just west of Conconully

There are no evacuation orders in place at this time.

Fire information and the incident command post is located at Omak Stampede Grounds in Omak, WA. Fire
information contact is Don Malone at (509) 822-8125 or you can also follow us on Facebook @NEWIMT3

fire to increase containment of the fire.

Status

9/8/2021 Update

Highlights: Yesterday’s hot and dry conditions allowed for active fire behavior, although successful firefighting
efforts resulted in limited fire growth. Firefighters were able to strengthen existing containment lines and continue
working towards establishing new lines. Today’s forecast calls for gusty southwest winds up to 27 mph, with the
potential to produce spot fires up to one quarter mile north from the fire’s edge near Stall Creek. Firefighters are
focusing their efforts on discovering and extinguishing any spot fires outside of the fire perimeter.

Operations: Fire on the northwestern perimeter continues to back downslope, coming within two hundred yards
of established fire line in some places. Helicopters are dropping water buckets and working together with ground
resources to slow the forward progression of the fire in this area, keeping it within the perimeter. Burnout
operations completed on Sunday near Stall Creek are holding and contributing to future containment lines.

Yesterday, smoke was increasingly visible in this area as unburned forest fuels in the interior burned around Stall
Creek. Crews are using long lengths of continuous fire hose installed along the fire line to extinguish any heat
that could threaten these lines. On the eastern edge near Bear Springs, helicopters dropped water on three
locations where the fire burned actively. Near Bitterroot Mountain, fire from the previous day’s burnout operation
continues to work its way across the landscape, consuming forest fuels and increasing the depth and strength of
control lines. If conditions are favorable, fire managers are planning to use aerial ignitions near Lynx Mountain in
the coming days to reinforce containment lines. No significant fire activity has been observed in the pre-existing
Brush Mountain spot fire or in the southern portions of the fire area, although firefighters remain vigilant in their

Yesterday: The smoke columns and glow from the fire after dark were visible from Chelan, Manson and
surrounding areas yesterday. These conditions resulted from additional hand and aerial firing operations
along the southern containment line and toward Stormy Mountain. Completion of these sections of line are
critical to the protection of homes in First Creek and keeping the fire from spreading toward the Entiat Valley.
Favorable conditions during the operations helped firefighters achieve a good amount of depth to fortify the
containment line.

Today: Firefighters are patrolling south of the fire where weather conditions this morning included gusty and
erratic winds that increased fire activity and potential for spots across the containment line. Firefighters are
poised to respond if any spot fires are detected. As conditions allow, burning operations will pick up from
where prior operations ended near Stormy Mountain last night and continue northwest towards Angle Peak,
to strengthen containment line along Devils Backbone. The deliberate and strategic pacing of the burnout is
influenced by terrain and weather.

Crews along the northwest side of the fire will mop up and secure line where burning operations took place
previously. These crews will also scout and evaluate opportunities to conduct ground and aerial ignitions to
reduce vegetation adjacent to the containment line on the west side of the fire. Due to increasing confidence
in containment lines on the east side of the fire, structure protection crews in the Lake Chelan corridor may
be breaking down and removing equipment in the Twenty-five Mile Creek area. They will work from north to
south along the corridor to the First Creek area over the next couple of days.

Weather and Fire Behavior: A weak weather disturbance passed over the area in the morning hours
resulting in brief and erratic gusty winds. Firefighters this morning will be on the lookout for spot fires that
may have ignited from embers associated with those winds. The forecast calls for full sunshine in the
afternoon with gusts along ridgetops reaching 20-25 mph. As smoke and clouds clear firefighters anticipate
favorable conditions for firing operations.
